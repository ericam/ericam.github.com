Poetry Doesn't Matter (but you should)

August 01, 2012 in art, minifesto, poetry, theatre.

A minifesto inspired by The Summer of Too Many Poets and an old-but-somewhat-prescient article about the dwindling influence of modern poetry.

The Medium is Irrelevant

The established rituals of the poetry world — the readings, small magazines, workshops, and conferences — exhibit a surprising number of self-imposed limitations. Why, for example, does poetry mix so seldom with music, dance, or theater?

I don't care about poetry. A medium is merely a description - a reduction used for analysis and critique, but counter to the spirit of creation. Forms adapt and die all the time, mere bi-products of the art.

Amanda Palmer isn't successful because "punk-influenced German-expressionist-cabaret keyboard" is a hit genre. She's not riding a wave, she is the wave. Apple didn't change the music industry with 8GB MP3 Players and digital downloads, both existed long before Apple got involved. People don't buy tablets, they buy iPads.

Only poets buy "poetry" - everyone else is buying the experience it provides. Stop selling the what, and start selling the why. Sell your aesthetic vision for the world and experiences that can never be forgotten.

Stop claiming that poetry should matter. Prove that you matter.

Art is for People

Like subsidized farming that grows food no one wants, a poetry industry has been created to serve the interests of the producers and not the consumers.

...

Poets must recapture the attention of the broader intellectual community by writing for nonspecialist publications. They must also avoid the jargon of contemporary academic criticism and write in a public idiom.

If you want an audience, learn their language. Learn what they care about and start talking to them. If all you care to write about is the ivory of your tower walls, then don't complain when no one cares to listen.

You don't build an audience by discussing the minutia of literary theory, or by complaining about the kids-these-days. You build an audience by finding people and mattering to them.

Take Control

You can blame your dwindling sales on the waning popularity of the medium, or the stupidity of your audience, but neither complaint will help you sell more art.

Take responsibility. Create new art (and new mediums) that have real impact on the lives of real people. Don't make a poem, make an experience.

Then sell it.

All the same can be said for theatre, electronic literature, classical music, dance, opera, and so on.
