Get Curious [Explosive Growth]

February 14, 2013 in art, fuck the muse, minifesto, process, writing.

[This is part 6 in the ongoing Fuck the Muse mini-series.]

Last week we started compiling our curiosity shopping lists. We’re entering a stage of explosive growth — pushing out from our seed in every direction — and it’s good to have some sense of what to look for. This week we’re going to do the shopping.

Research » Steal » Invent

Too often we split into genre-camps, with the documentarians doing research, the remix artists stealing and sampling, and everyone else struggling to create from scratch. I don’t care which approach will dominate your final piece — it’s not time for that yet — right now you need to collect all the material you can, no matter what format it takes.

You need to fill your head (and your notebook) with possibilities.

I find it most helpful to start with loose research, putting ideas into my head without too much concern for the details. You aren’t working on an academic paper here, you’re just filling the tanks. Your creative mind works by copying, transforming, & combining, so the more ideas you put into your head, the more you can get out when it’s time.

Get your facts first, and then you can distort them as much as you please.

— Mark Twain

Use your shopping list as a guide. When you read/see/hear something you think might fit, take it. Clip it out of an article, take a photo, make a playlist, or just take notes. When ideas start to merge and you are inspired to say something new, set aside the research and invent your own material.

These things happen in cycles, and you’ll get farthest by jumping between them as you are inspired. If research isn’t working, try inventing something. If you can’t think of anything to invent, find something to steal.

Contain Multitudes

Many artists have a deep fear of being un-original. Let go of it right now. It’s a deadly, self-fullfilling profecy. Here’s what happens:

  1. You don’t want to be un-original, so you don’t research or steal anything.
  2. All you have to work with are the stereotypes and cliches already in your head.
  3. Everything you invent is predictable.

Don’t rely on your own pre-conceptions. Look in other genres and other mediums, fill your notebook with voices that are not your own, and then fill your artwork with contradictoins and suprises. Embrace the chaos of multitudes contained inside one simple seed.
